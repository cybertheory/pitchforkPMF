What Pitchfork
PMF is not

  • ✗ A definition of PMF — that debate belongs to economists, VCs, and grumpy wizards.
  • ✗ A guarantee — markets shift, customers surprise you, life casts its own spells. No potion included.
  • ✗ A one-time ritual — it's a living compass, not a checkbox. You don't cast this spell once.
Our goal: give founders a reliable framework — a compass to manage the balance beam of all the moving parts, and ensure you're always moving toward an idea that actually generates revenue.
The Framework — Why "Pitchfork" 🔱
Three Prongs.
One Direction.
Prong 01
Technology
The core innovation — the invention, protocol, framework, or design approach that underlies your entire business. The stem. The thing only you have (or uniquely combined).
Prong 02
Business
The key outcome — the specific workflow you're optimizing, the specific pain you're eradicating. The dollars. The reason anyone opens their wallet on a Tuesday.
Prong 03
Product
The materialization — the shape of your technology in the world. How your idea slots into existing workflows. The UX. The thing people actually touch.
The one incantation you must memorize 🔮
Technology
≠ Product
Your technology is not your product. The product automates the workflows around the technology — making integration seamless, invisible, effortless.

The product is the UX. It is not the key innovation. Don't confuse the two, or the cauldron boils over and you end up building a demo with no users.
Where You Start Matters
Two kinds of founders.
One pitchfork. 🔱

📖 Starts with Business

You have the outcome, the pain, the market. You're holding the spellbook but need someone to conjure the technology that makes it real.

→ Find the tech layer that unlocks the business you already see clearly.

⚗️ Starts with Technology

You brewed the potion. Now you need someone to drink it. Most discourage "tech-first" — but it's been done, many times, by wizards just like you.

→ Find ONE thin workflow. Anchor there first. The other 47 use-cases can wait.
How to Apply Pitchfork PMF
Two steps.
That's it.
1

Identify all three axes

Name your Technology, Business, and Product in one sentence each. If any one is fuzzy, conflated with another, or answered with "well it depends" — you don't have PMF. Write it down. Now.

2

Validate each axis separately

Technology: Does it work? Business: Do people really feel the pain? Does solving it provide real lift? Product: Does the shape fit the workflow? Iterate — user interviews, data, relentless obsession.

→

The key insight 💡

Once Technology and Business are validated, Product is a navigable problem. The iteration is the job, not the failure. Find people willing to work with you — and build.

Case Study 01 / 07
PostHog B2B
Open-source product analytics — the one that lives in your infra, not theirs
Technology
Open-source, self-hostable event pipeline — analytics, feature flags, session replay, A/B tests in one data layer. No third-party cloud required, no GDPR nightmare fuel.
Business
Engineering teams hate sending customer behavioral data to five different SaaS vendors. PostHog kills the privacy-vs-insight tradeoff entirely. One tool, your servers.
Product
All-in-one product OS: funnels, heatmaps, feature flags, A/B tests in one interface. Deploys in minutes, baked into the dev workflow rather than bolted on top.
Pitchfork lesson: The event pipeline is not the product. The workflow that replaces five SaaS subscriptions with one self-hosted dashboard is what gets engineers to evangelize it internally.
Case Study 02 / 07
Supabase B2B
Open-source Firebase — with SQL, like God intended
Technology
PostgreSQL — battle-tested, relational — wrapped with a real-time engine, auto-generated REST & GraphQL APIs, row-level security, and edge functions. Not invented. Masterfully orchestrated.
Business
Devs need a backend fast. Firebase locked you into proprietary NoSQL. Supabase offers full SQL power with zero vendor lock-in and a free tier that actually works.
Product
One dashboard provisions auth, database, storage, and edge functions in minutes. The UX deliberately mirrors Firebase — collapsing the migration cost to near zero.
Pitchfork lesson: Supabase didn't invent Postgres. They productized the workflow around it so well that devs feel 10× faster. Technology ≠ Product, exhibit B.
Case Study 03 / 07
Brex B2B
Corporate cards for startups — no personal credit, no adult supervision required
Technology
Real-time underwriting using non-traditional signals — cash balance, investor backing, burn rate — bypassing personal credit history. The banks never thought to do this.
Business
Startups couldn't get corporate cards. The legacy credit system excluded them structurally. Brex found a massive, creditworthy segment left entirely on the table.
Product
A modern spend OS — cards, expense management, bill pay — shaped precisely around how startups actually spend. Not a bank. A founder's financial command center.
Pitchfork lesson: The underwriting model is not the product. The entire spend workflow designed for startup DNA is what drove adoption, retention, and enterprise expansion.
Case Study 04 / 07
Vercel Prosumer
Push to deploy. That's it. That's the product.
Technology
Edge-first deployment infrastructure — instant global CDN, serverless functions at the edge, preview deployments on every git push. Git as the deployment primitive.
Business
Frontend devs ship fast but deploy slow. DevOps friction kills momentum. Vercel eliminates the entire "getting it live" tax — from push to production in under 30 seconds.
Product
Zero-config deploys, branch previews, analytics, Web Vitals — all invisible until needed. Built by Next.js creators: the framework and platform share the same DNA.
Pitchfork lesson: The CDN is not the product. The experience of pushing code and watching it go live globally in 30 seconds is why developers evangelize Vercel to their entire company.
Case Study 05 / 07
Firecrawl 🕷️ B2B
Turn any website into LLM-ready data. No headless browser nightmares required.
Technology
Web crawling engine that renders JS-heavy pages, handles auth flows, and outputs clean Markdown structured for LLM consumption. A very polite, very fast spider.
Business
AI teams building RAG pipelines waste enormous time writing scrapers that break on Monday mornings. Firecrawl is the reliable data acquisition layer nobody wants to build themselves.
Product
One API call: send a URL, receive clean Markdown. Handles crawl depth, sitemaps, rate limits, JS rendering automatically. Plugs into any AI pipeline in under 10 minutes.
Pitchfork lesson: The scraper engine is not the product. The one-line API that makes any website a data source for your AI — without ever touching a config file — is what commands the price point.
Case Study 06 / 07
Cursor ✨ Prosumer
The AI code editor that doesn't make you feel stupid for using AI
Technology
Deep codebase indexing + frontier LLM integration — the editor understands your entire repo as context, not just the open file. Agentic edits, explain, refactor at repo scale.
Business
Developers lose hours context-switching, hunting docs, writing boilerplate. Cursor collapses the gap between intent and implementation — coding feels like thinking out loud.
Product
A VS Code fork — zero learning curve. AI is woven into every keystroke: inline completions, repo-aware chat, multi-file edits. It accelerates the workflow you already have.
Pitchfork lesson: The LLM integration is not the product. The familiar environment where AI feels native, not bolted on is why Cursor has cult-level retention. Comfort is an actual moat.
Case Study 07 / 07
Lovable 💖 B2C
Build a full-stack app with a prompt. No terminal. No trauma. No excuses.
Technology
LLM-driven full-stack code generation — React frontend, Supabase backend, deployed to the web — produced from natural language in an iterative conversation. Genuinely witchcraft.
Business
Non-technical founders had ideas but no engineering budget. Validating an idea cost 3 months and $50K. Lovable collapses that to an afternoon and the price of a nice dinner.
Product
A chat interface that builds, previews, and deploys a real app with auth, database, and a live URL. The IDE is completely invisible. The code exists — you just never have to see it.
Pitchfork lesson: The code generator is not the product. The journey from idea to live app without ever opening a terminal — that psychological unlock — makes non-technical founders weep tears of joy.
The Final Spell 🔱
The compass,
not the destination.
  • ◆ Identify your three axes — Technology, Business, Product — clearly and separately. If they blur together, keep digging.
  • ◆ Validate each one. Until validated, they're assumptions. Assumptions are spells that haven't been cast yet.
  • ◆ Technology ≠ Product. Your invention is the engine. The product is the car the customer actually drives off the lot.
  • ◆ Markets shift. Moving parts keep moving. Return to the framework whenever you feel lost in the woods. 🌲
  • ◆ Once Tech + Business are solid — Product is just iteration. Find your first users. Pick up the pitchfork. Build.
